# Why Omoluabi

*Omoluabi* is a Yoruba idea: a person who acts with integrity, honor, and responsibility — someone accountable to a community and to those who come after. The project carries that name because it asks the same thing of a tool that the culture asks of a person.

This page explains why Omoluabi emerged from a long working life rather than from a single idea.

---

## A journalism background

I spent formative years helping build digital editorial infrastructure as online publishing was being invented — work across major newsrooms, and online newsrooms I built and directed while living in Nigeria. That work was about the same questions Omoluabi now asks at the level of a system: Where did this come from? Who verified it? What context is missing? Who is it for?

## Accessibility, learned firsthand

While in Nigeria, a severe case of drug-resistant malaria triggered a neurological illness that became Guillain-Barré syndrome and later chronic inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy (CIDP). It changed how I move through the world and how I think about tools.

Accessibility stopped being a feature to add and became the question underneath everything: who is a system actually built for, and whose participation does it quietly assume away. That question runs through all of the work, Omoluabi included.

## Artist support and archives

Supporting artists — most directly as Head of Artist Initiatives & Inclusion at Eyebeam — taught me that the hardest part of public-interest work is rarely the technology. It is care: holding people's material, their context, and their consent without flattening any of it. Archives and cultural memory are not storage problems. They are problems of trust over time.

## Information systems and AI literacy

As editorial work became algorithmic, two things became clear: most people are handed AI systems they cannot interrogate, and most documentation tools extract first and ask permission never. Omoluabi is a response to both — built to make AI legible and to keep a human in the loop where meaning is decided.

## Civic documentation

Journalists, researchers, students, and community observers all gather signals from the world and try to hold them honestly. They need tools that track provenance, surface missing context, and translate across languages and time — without surrendering control of what they noticed.

---

## What Omoluabi is

Omoluabi works from a single principle: **sovereignty over observation.** The person who noticed something should control how it is held, when it moves, what form it takes, and who benefits. Omoluabi is the infrastructure that keeps the interval open — for consent, context, and care — on terms set by the observer, not the system.

It takes two connected forms: a portable **device** built for the field, and a **web engine** that can be used on its own. Together they support five plain actions — **observe, verify, translate, preserve, share** — and a vocabulary of evidence states that says, out loud, how sure we are and on what terms something can move:

```
Human observed   ·   Machine suggested   ·   Context missing   ·   Consent unclear
Protected uncertainty   ·   Unsafe to publish   ·   Ready for review   ·   Withheld
```

That is the bridge: decades of editorial work, an accessibility practice learned in the body, years of supporting artists and archives — all of it pointing at the same need, and Omoluabi built to meet it.
